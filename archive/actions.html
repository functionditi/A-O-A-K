ACTIONS



GESTURES

Controlling the Flow:
By adjusting the pressure between the fingers, the artist controls the steady trickle of powder. The thumb acts as a gatekeeper, regulating the amount released.

Refilling Technique:
Without interrupting the flow, the artist skillfully replenishes the powder by scooping more into the hand, maintaining the same grip.

Smooth Movements:
The hand moves in fluid motions to create straight lines, curves, and loops. The steadiness of the hand ensures clean, continuous lines.

Wrist and Arm Coordination:
The wrist provides flexibility for intricate curves, while the arm moves guide larger strokes and transitions across the design.

Pressure Variation:
Subtle changes in finger pressure can adjust the thickness of the lines, adding depth and variation to the pattern.

POSTURE

Stance:
Artists may stand, squat, or sit while drawing, depending on the size of the kolam and personal comfort.

Balance:
Maintaining a stable posture is crucial, as the artist often stretches to reach different parts of the design, and attempts to not smudge the kolam!

Breath Control:
Some artists synchronize their breathing with their movements, enhancing focus and steadiness.

Rhythmic Motion:
Experienced artists develop a rhythm in their gestures, which contributes to the symmetry and harmony of the kolam.

Memory and Improvisation:
While many designs are traditional, artists often draw from memory or improvise, relying on muscle memory and intuition in their hand movements.




ADAPTATION

Alternative Materials:
While traditional kolams are drawn freehand with powder, some artists use tools like stencils or pens for precision, especially in contemporary adaptations.

Innovations:
Modern variations might involve colored powders or paints, requiring adjustments in hand techniques to accommodate different textures and mediums.

MATERIALS

Rice Flour:
Rice flour is the primary material for creating kolams, trickled between the middle and index fingers with the thumb guiding the flow

Chalk:
In urban settings where rice flour might be less practical due to foot traffic, chalk sticks or chalk powder is used.

Synthetic Color:
Colored kolams are particularly popular during festivals like Pongal, Diwali, and weddings.



EXPERIMENT

Exploring Kolams as an outcome of the artist's in-the-moment interactions with the medium, and further exploring how alternate forms of tangible interaction (e.g. a Wii Nunchuck) can result in alternate forms of a Kolam. Furthermore, investigating how the improvisational hand movements used in kolam-making can be captured and translated into digital inputs.
